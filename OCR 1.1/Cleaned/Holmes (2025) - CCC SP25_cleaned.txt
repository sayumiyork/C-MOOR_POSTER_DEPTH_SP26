Melanogenesis within the Drosophila Midgut:
Neuromelanin and Dopamine

================================================================================
SECTION: AUTHORS
================================================================================

Jesse Holmes, Rosa Alcazar

================================================================================
SECTION: ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This research was focused in genes connected to melanogenesis (synthesis of
melanin) and its antioxidant uses. It more specifically deals with
melanogenesis within the midgut of Drosophila and the connection to
melanogenesis in humans. Within Drosophila a gene that regulates its mating
behaviors and pigmentation is known as “Y™ or “yellow”. It belongs to a
family of genes that regulate pigmentation and dopamine. When the yellow
gene is silenced it will cause a lack of pigmentation and alterations to mating
behavior. (Massey, 2019) The research in this poster focused in on the Fe
section of the Drosophila midgut. Once the data we obtained from Marianes’
study was processed using SciServers we determined that the yellow gene
was significantly expressed in the Fe region. A paralog and ortholog gene
found along the way was a gene called RGN/SMP30. It regulated calcium ion
levels which is integral to melanogenesis. This was expressed highly in the I
human gastrointestinal tract. Through connecting our results to findings from
Hedges’ research on irons effect on melanogenesis in human brains we
conclude that the reason yellow was highly expressed in the Fe region was
likely to coat dopamine to protect it from oxidative stress (neuromelanin).

SECTION: METHODS
================================================================================

There is an intricate relationship to melanin expression and dopamine in
most organisms. Humans have many specialized expressions of
melanogenesis, some of which for skin, others for more neural functions.
Drosophila have a unique expression of genes regulating melanogenesis too.
The “yellow” gene regulates cuticle and larval mouthpart pigmentation.
Yellow likely has neural functions too. (Biessman H. 1985) Many papers
referenced go over the many details of a type of melanin called
neuromelanin. Neuromelanin is a type of melanin used to coat
neurotransmitters such as dopamine to protect it from oxidative stress. It is
my hypothesis that the Yellow gene is expressed highly in the Fe region of
the midgut because it has other characteristics beyond pigmentation, likely
involving neuromelanin. We will use R data analysis on Marianes’ dataset of
the Drosophila midgut to determine any significance of the Yellow gene's
differential expression between the regions. Through that we will do further
research into its significant paralogs and orthologs.

- Using the R coding language from a notebook located on Sci-Severs. - Sci-Servers database on fly midgut expression from Marianes’ research - Various research papers published on the National Library of Medicine so
we can see how melanin and dopamine operates in certain environments - Graphs Supporting Connection such as Figure 4 and 5 coming from
referenced research articles

SECTION: DISCUSSION
================================================================================

Neuromelanin production is amplified by iron ions which is a probable
reason as to why it is expressed in the iron region of the Drosophila midgut.
The paralog and human ortholog, RGN/SMP30, is not differentially
expressed but its presence can be theorized to be involved in regulating
calcium levels throughout the midgut to allow for biosynthesis of melanin
and other chemicals. My hypothesis is generally supported but it can be
further confirmed and potentially debunked with more intensive studies. It is
probable that dopamine is produced in multiple spots within the midgut and
gets coated in the Fe region to protect it from oxidative stress. This is not
absolutely proven but there is good evidence to support it such as Figure 4
and 5 which shows the rate of melanogenesis within the human brain in the
presence of iron. (Hedges 2020) This can be completely different in other
systems such as the gut but it potentially could be a similar process. The only
change in the system within the human brain production of neuromelanin was
the change in iron ion concentration. (Hedges 2020) Dysregulated
Le omalania expression is linked to many human neurodegenerative
disorders such as Parkinson's. (Moreno-Garcia 2021) This could explain the
flies inability to properly mate. Research into neuromelanin within
Drill can gives us a better idea into the genes directly involved. It can
potentially help us identify patterns for human research into
neurodegenerative symptoms caused by dysregulated neuromelanin.
