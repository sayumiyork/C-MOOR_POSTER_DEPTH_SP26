SECTION: ABSTRACT

‘Trehalose is a critical sugar in Drosophila melanogaster, in which its breakdown by
trehalase provides glucose for energy metabolism. The pathway of trehalose
catabolism involves two transporters (TRET1/GLUT1) and one (TREH) enzyme. In
the midgut. In this study, we tested the hypothesis that TREH, TRET1, and GLUT1
would show comparable regional expression. We analyzed gene expression
patterns across midgut regions. TREH was highly expressed in the anterior and
posterior midgut, TRET1 was enriched in the copper cell region, and GLUT1 was
concentrated in central digestive regions with TREH activity. These results indicate
that, contrary to our hypothesis, expression of trehalose catabolism genes is
regionally specialized rather than uniform. Our findings highlight the organization
of the trehalose catabolism pathway, demonstrating that trehalose uptake,
hydrolysis, and glucose. Transport across different regions in the midgut. This
study provides insight into the regulation of carbohydrate metabolism in flies and
parallel sugar processing in humans.

SECTION: INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

Trehalose is a major sugar in insects. TREH breaks trehalose into glucose in the Drosophila
midgut. Studies show that the midgut is divided into regions that carry out different
digestive roles. These regions differ in pH, metal levels, and nutrient handling. Transport
genes for trehalose and glucose respond to these regional differences, which create
distinct expression patterns across the gut. The knowledge gap is the relationship
between TREH expression and transport genes linked to trehalose and glucose
movement. We do not yet know if these genes follow similar regional patterns or if the
midgut separates digestion and transport across compartments. Our scientific question
asks whether TREH, TRET1, and GLUT 1 show similar expression across the anterior and
posterior midgut. Our hypothesis predicts similar patterns because these genes
participate in linked steps of trehalose breakdown and glucose transport. Drosophilais a
strong model for this work because its midgut is well mapped, its genetics are accessible
and its carbohydrate metabolism is similar to over organisms, including humans.

SECTION: RESULTS
================================================================================

The gene plot across the region graph from figure 1 showed TREH is most expressed in the anterior
and posterior regions. It matched supporting information because these regions are where
macromolecule breakdown (Trehalose Catabolism) and absorption occur. Significant gene
expression in the Cu region was observed for TRET1, but for GLUT, the higher expression was for
the posterior region. The differential gene expression revealed the differences between each
pathway. TRET 1's expression was due to the region's acidity and the need for trehalose to be
transported into the cell into a more stable environment to be broken down. Trehalose does not
function well in this region. GLUT 1 expression was because it is the final stage where absorption
occurs and glucose is moved into cells to be absorbed. Trehalase doesn't function well in this region
due to the acidity, and the trehalose must be transported inside the cell (a more stable environment)
to be broken down.

SECTION: DISCUSSION
================================================================================

The gene TREH (Trehalase) is expressed in the midgut of Drosophila melanogaster, where
it breaks down trehalose into glucose. Our analysis shows that TRET1, the trehalose
transporter, is highly expressed in the copper cell region, suggesting that trehalose uptake
is specialized in this area. This supports local metabolic or metal handling functions. In
contrast, GLUT? is a glucose transporter that is enriched in the far posterior digestive
regions of the midgut. While we initially hypothesized that all genes in the trehalose
catabolism pathway would show similar expression patterns, our data indicate a regionally
specialized organization in TRET1 and GLUT? expressed in distinct midgut regions,
reflecting their specific roles in transporting sugars. These findings highlight the
importance of the enzyme transporter region in the midgut, providing insight into how
carbohydrate metabolism is tuned in flies and offering similar data to sugar processing in
other organisms, including humans. Future work could investigate how alternating TREH,
TRET1, or GLUT? expression in specific midgut regions affects sugar metabolism, as well
as additional trehalose pathway genes to complete the map of the pathway.
