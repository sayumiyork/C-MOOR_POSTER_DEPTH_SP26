Analysis Of Gene Expression Of Acids Reflux Related Genes Across The Midgut

SECTION: AUTHORS

================================================================================
SECTION: ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This study explores gene expression related to acid reflux-associated pathways in the Drosophila
melanogaster midgut, it focus on acidification processes that influence metabolic gene regulation. These
processes are closely regulated by gut-brain sensory signaling, which adapts feeding behavior throughout the
fly’s life cycle. Fundamental to this investigation is the gene vhal6-1 (FBgn0262736) with the human gene
ortholog being ATP6VOC, which encodes a subunit of the vacuolar H'-ATPase (V-ATPase), necessary for
acidifying the midgut—especially within the copper (Cu) cell region. The gene functions by translating
V-ATPase, which is a channel protein that encourage multiple functions. Within this we identified paralogs to
our gene of research, in order to understand its expression throughout the phagosome pathway. This localized
acidification plays a vital role in digestion and maintaining metabolic homeostasis. Loss of vhal6-1 function
disrupts pH balance in the gut and leads to metabolic dysfunction. Concerning ts regulation by neural and
hormonal signaling pathways, vhal6-1 emerges as a core integrator of digestive processes and systemic
metabolic control, when identified along the phagosome pathway. These findings highlight conserved
biological mechanisms that may provide clarification into human gastric acid disorders such as acid reflux.

================================================================================
SECTION: INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

Acid reflux is a condition shown in millions of people in the world and results in pain in the esophagus due to the backflow of stomach
acid. To better comprehend the molecular mechanisms behind this condition, our study analyzes gene expression patterns in various
regions of the Drosophila melanogaster gut, to concentrate on the copper (Cu) region. We chose Drosophila as our model organism due to
its well-annotated genome and the conservation of many biological pathways with humans, making it a powerful system for studying gut
signaling processes relevant to acid reflux. We hypothesize that Vhal6-1 (Vacuolar H+ ATPase 16kD subunit 1), our gene of interest, will
show the highest expression in the Cu region. This is based on its known role in important signaling pathways and previous research
highlighting the Cu region's significance in gut function and regulation. After further analyzing the pathways we found Vhal6-1 to be
found in the phagosome pathway. Phagosomes function by filtering biological debris in the cell, which can be reduced with the mhibition
of our gene. Although Vhal6-1 doesn’t technically have a direct human relative, the pathways it is involved in are highly related in order
to explore the processes involving acid reflux due to the genetic and cellular processes. Our research focus is to apply the unknown in the
current discussion and lay the foundations for future studies that could eventually lead to more understanding and treatment of acid reflux.

SECTION: DISCUSSION
================================================================================

There is clear evidence that the effects of acid reflux are influenced by
genetic factors. Our research focused on identifying specific regions
within the Drosophila melanogaster midgut that express human-relevant
genes associated with this condition. We discovered that the gene Vhal 6-1
is expressed in the al and Cu regions of the midgut, areas involved in the
phagosome pathway. This expression highlights a potential genetic
contributor to pH regulation in the gut, which may underlie the acidic
conditions characteristic of acid reflux.Additional genes found to be
expressed in this region—VhaPPAl-1 (Figure 2) and ATPsynC (Figure
3)—are paralogs of Vhal6-1, further supporting the functional
significance of this pathway. The human ortholog of Vhal6-1 is
ATP6VOC, suggesting a conserved mechanism between species. Our
findings provide valuable insight into the genetic basis of acid reflux and
its expression in the midgut using the Drosophila model, thereby
contributing to our understanding of its connection to metabolic disorders.
Understanding how the phagosome pathway and Cu region influence gene
expression may help clarify the genetic regulation of gut homeostasis.
