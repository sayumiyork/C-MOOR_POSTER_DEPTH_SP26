================================================================================

The methyl-CpG binding protein 2 (MECP2) gene is essential for neuronal

nervous system. It is a critical regulator of neuronal gene expression as it is a key role
in modulating the expression of gamma-aminobutyric acid type A receptor subunit
beta 3 (Gabrb3) and brain derived neurotrophic factor (Bdnf). Mutstions in MECP2
are linked to Rett syndrome and related neurodevelopmental disorders; however, the

levels were quantified and visualized using normalized counts and differential
expression analysis. The results indicate that MECP2 is broadly expressed
throughout the mouse brain, with notably higher expression in the cortex and
hippocampus compared to subcortical regions. The Gabrb3 genes match expression
in the same regions, while Bdnf shows higher expression in the prefrontal cortex
These findings support the hypothesis that MECP2 expression is associated in many

insight into how dysregulstion of this gene may contribute to neurological dysfunction.

SECTION: DISCUSSION
================================================================================

Because MECP2 is expressed across the brain, its loss disrupts activity in

Cells with faulty MECP2 showed changes in how tightly DNA was packed. Areas
normally kept “closed” by MECF2 became more open, suggesting that MECP2
was no longer controlling gene activity properly. People with harmful MECP2
mutations showed common Rett syndrome traits, such as slowed development.
Mutations that caused the biggest drop in MECP2 function were linked to more
severe sympioms.

This body of work in mice places MECP2 st the center of a regulatory network
that keeps Bdnf and Gabrb3 balanced along with other genes in the brain.
Together they provide a picture of how normal and abnormal functions work and
can help shape opportunities for future research. Further experiments in MECP2
expression can one day be characterized and may open up understanding of
new pathways and its influence on other neurological dysfunctions.
