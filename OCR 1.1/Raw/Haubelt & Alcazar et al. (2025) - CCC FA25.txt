SECTION: TITLE
================================================================================

Exploring DRD2: The Association of PD Risk with Dopamine
Receptors in the Dopaminergic Synapse

================================================================================
SECTION: AUTHORS
================================================================================

Samantha Haubelt, Rosa Alcazar

================================================================================
SECTION: ABSTRACT
================================================================================

I was interested in the genes, pathways, and mechanisms related
to the neuropsychiatric disorder Parkinson's Disease and wanted
to investigate the specific genes involved in the dopaminergic
synapse. Abnormal variants of the Drd2 gene has been implicated
in many neurological disorders including Parkinson's Disease in
humans (Noble, E). The dopaminergic synapse involves genes,
including tyrosine hydroxylase (Th), catechol-O-methyltransferase
(Comt), dopamine receptor D2 (Drd2), and dopa decarboxylase
(Ddc). These genes provide instructions for making enzymes
responsible for regulating dopamine levels and are differentially
expressed in the striatum part of the mice brain. The striatum is a
critical component of the brain that controls motor, reward, and
executive function (Bamford, I, N). This indicates that the
correlation between these genes and neuropsychiatric disorders
including Parkinson's Disease. The differential expression and
KEGG Map data further support the relation and offer possible
treatments or regulation.

================================================================================
SECTION: INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

Many neuropsychiatric disorders including Parkinson's
Disease, Schizophrenia, and Post-traumatic Stress Disorder are
seen to have direct correlation to dopamine receptor D2 (Drd2)
and its variants. The dopaminergic synapse pathways releases

the neurotransmitter dopamine, which binds to dopamine
receptors, including Drd2, on the postsynaptic neuron located
in the striatum (STR) to influence various brain functions.
Many research articles express that people suffering from
Parkinson's Disease show symptoms including rest tremor,
bradykinesia, rigidity and loss of postural reflexes (Jankovic,
J). I wanted to investigate the expression of genes involved in
the dopaminergic synapse pathways in the STR and how they
affect the increased risk of Parkinson's Disease using mice as
the model system due to their close genetic and physiological
similarity to humans

SECTION: DISCUSSION
================================================================================

Studies have shown that people who suffer from parkinson’s
disease show symptoms of rest tremor, bradykinesia
(slowness of movement), rigidity and loss of postural reflexes.
These symptoms are closely related to the function of the
striatum and are related to the dopaminergic synapse. This
indicates that genes differentially expressed the striatum can
have a possible role in Parkinson's Disease and its symptoms.
The uniform distribution of expression shown by all the
related genes indicate the involvement of the portion of the
brain responsible for movement (including voluntary or
involuntary) and coordination. Therefore focusing attention on
the genes involved in the dopaminergic synapse in the brain
can help us better understand neuropsychiatric disorders like
Parkinson's Disease. Observing differential expression in the
brain, specifically the striatum with mice as the model system
due to their close genetic and physiological similarity to
humans, will illustrate what cells or mechanisms are
expressed and show their role in the relationship between the
dopaminergic synapse and neuropsychiatric disorders.
