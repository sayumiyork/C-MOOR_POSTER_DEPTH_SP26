Macromolecule Breakdown and Absorption: Analysis of
Differential Expression Using Single Cell and Bulk RNA-seq Data

================================================================================
SECTION: AUTHORS
================================================================================

================================================================================
SECTION: ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Productive macromolecule breakdown relies on certain genes expressed in
specific regions of the Drosophila midgut. Understanding differential gene
expression in particular regions can uncover processes relevant to human
digestion as well as metabolic disorders. Our researched aimed to compare
the regional expression patterns of two genes both involved in starch and
sucrose metabolism using single-cell as well as bulk RNA-sequencing
datasets.

After analyzing distinct genes responsible for starch and sucrose metabolism,
we hypothesized that Amy-P and Amyrel would be enriched in the same
region of the midgut. We found that Amy-P, a gene known to be associated
with the hydrolysis of dietary starch, is significantly differentially expressed in
the Al region of the Drosophila midgut, which is involved with carbohydrate
metabolism(Marianes, A. & Spradling, A. C. 2013). Unexpectedly, the Amyrel
gene was enriched in the P1 region of the midgut which is responsible for
absorbing nutrients from digested food(Marianes, A., & Spradling, A. C. 2013).
Although these findings contradicted our hypothesis, they indicate that
starch and sucrose are spatially separated rather than co-localized. This
organization may allow for sequential processing of carbohydrates potentially
suggest that spatial separation prevents wasted enzyme activity. Overall, our
work highlights the importance of combining single-cell and bulk RNA-seq
data, in order to identify gene expression patterns of and its possible
relevance to the digestive biology of humans.

SECTION: INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

The Drosophila Melanogaster js a species of fruit fly that is commonly used as
a_model organism in laboratory settings. This is due to the species high
reproduction rate, short lifespan, cost effectiveness, as well as the numerous
similarities the Drosophila model holds in comparison to humans. It is
through the study of the Drosophila Melanogaster that we have been able to
better understand the molecular complexities and health conditions in other
organisms. Thanks to the genetic similarities displayed between the
Drosophila model and humans, we are able to use data collected from these
simplistic organisms and apply that to human medicine. For our research, we
compared two segments of the Drosophila midgut and sought out which
genes are predominately expressed. The segments that we compared against
included the Al and P2_4 regions of the midgut. The Al, or anterior region,_is
tasked with macromolecule breakdown while the P2_4& segment, or posterior
region, controls molecule absorption (Marianes, A, & Spradling, A. C. (2013). We
found that our selected gene, Amy-p, showed high levels of genetic
expression in the Al region of the midgut as well as being highly expressed in
pancreatic tissue in humans. This led us to hypothesize that all genes within
the amylase family would share a similar function given their lineage. Upon
further analysis, we found that each gene within the amylase family serve
varying roles in molecular degradation and absorption. Their expression
throughout a given system varies depending on the specific gene and targets
differing tissues, rather than those pertaining the pancreas as seen with

SECTION: METHODS
================================================================================

Programs and websites utilized during our research include:

ElyBase: Allowed for identification of specific flybase gene IDs. General information pertaining to
the gene such as location, function, and expression. It also displayed shared orthologs between
the Drosophila Melanogaster model and humans.

Human Protein Atlas: Gathering human orthologs of our specified gene, the Human Protein Atlas
allowed for us to view data on the human equivalent to our chosen gene. Similarly to the FlyBase,
information including location of the gene, function, and expression of the gene could be
determined.

Anvil: This program allowed for access to a RNA-sequencing library. With R-coding, our group was
able to view relevant expression data of a variety of genes. This program allowed for a broad
analysis of related genes and helped to narrow down our findings to a gene that met our criteria.

SECTION: RESULTS
================================================================================

RNA-sequencing analysis conducted through Anvil revealed that the FBgn0O000079 and
FBgn0020506 genes exhibit highly region-specific expression across the Drosophila melanogaster
midgut.

Through R-coding, the function plotAcrossRegions demonstrated that the EBgn0000079 gene is
strongly expressed in the Al region, with very low expression in posterior midgut zones such as
P2_4 (Figure 3) and that the EBgn0020506 gene is strongly expressed in the P1 region compared
to Al (Figure 4). The function formatDESeq2Results(temp), was then used to determine the
differential expression between Al and P2_4 for FBgn000079, and between Al and P1 for
EBgn0020506. This analysis showed an adjusted p-value of 2.0197766e*” for FBgn0000079 and of
3.364346e° for FBgn0020506 (Figures 5 and 6). These values are far below 0.05, indicating the
differences in expression are statistically significant and unlikely to have occurred by random
chance.

SECTION: DISCUSSION
================================================================================

The RNA-seq analysis showed that FBgn0O000079, also referred to as Amy-p, is highly and
significantly expressed in the Al region of the Drosophila melanogaster midgut, compared
to the lower expression in the posterior P2_4 region. Because the Al region is associated
with macromolecule digestion and the P2_4& region is more specialized for molecule
absorption (RNA-seq: Fruit-fly, 2019), these expression differences align with the known
function of FBgnO0O00079. The gene encodes alpha-amylase, an enzyme that catalyzes
starch hydrolysis, the breakdown of starch into glucose and maltose through the addition of
water molecules (Gene: Dmel\Amy-p, 2025). The extremely low adjusted p-value further
supports the conclusion that this region-specific expression is meaningful and not due to
random variation.

In addition, the RNA-seq analysis showed that Amyrel (FBgn0020506), another member of
the amylase family, is highly expressed in the posterior P1 region compared to low
expression in the anterior Al region. Biochemical studies indicate that Amyrel is not limited
to starch hydrolysis, as it also “produces a complex mixture of maltooligosaccharides”
before generating glucose and maltose, which is “in sharp contrast with canonical animal
a-amylases” (Feller et al, 2021). This demonstrates that even when an amylase gene is
expressed in a digestive region, it may perform several other functions beyond simple
breakdown.

To extend these findings to a broader biological context, we examined the human ortholog
AMY2B, which also encodes an alpha-amylase enzyme involved in carbohydrate digestion.
According to the Human Protein Atlas, AMY2B is pancreas-enriched and functions primarily
in digestive enzyme secretion. It has also been reported as a prognostic marker in rectum
adenocarcinoma, suggesting a potential association with human gastrointestinal disease.
While Drosophila and human digestive systems differ structurally, the shared enzymatic
role of Amy-p and AMY2B highlights how model organisms can provide insights into gene
function that may be relevant to human digestive biology and disease.

Overall, the results obtained from the RNA-seq analysis challenge our initial hypothesis that
all amylase-family genes share a similar function. Instead, these results reveal functional
diversity within the amylase-family, with Amy-p primarily contributing to molecule
breakdown in the anterior region, and Amyrel contributing to carbohydrate modification in
the posterior region (Feller et al, 2021). This indicates that amylase-family genes are not
limited to molecular breakdown and reinforces the observation that carbohydrate
processing in the midgut is a compartmentalized process. To determine whether the
functional differences between Amyrel and Amy-P resulted from compartment-specific
evolutionary pressures within the midgut, additional biochemical study is needed. Similarly,
further clinical and experimental evidence will be necessary to clarify the specific role of
AMY2B in rectum adenocarcinoma.
