================================================================================

Ras85D Expression in Drosophila Midgut Highlights a
~~ Model for KRAS-Driven Colon Cancer

================================================================================
SECTION: AUTHORS
================================================================================

Katya Paramo-QOjeda, Randeep Johal, Rosa Alcazar

================================================================================
SECTION: ABSTRACT
================================================================================

KRAS mutations drive many human cancers by promoting
uncontrolled cell growth. Ras85D, the Drosophila homolog of
KRAS, allows for studying Ras pathway regulation in a simple
model system. We found that Ras85D is highly expressed and
differentially regulated in the Drosophila midgut, especially in
the Copper region. Other genes in the cell cycle regulation,
including BRCA2, APC, Pi3K92E (PIK3CA), MLH]1, and PMS2,
showed no significant regional differences. The KRAS gene
encodes a protein that controls cell growth signals. When
mutated, it becomes permanently active, causing uncontrolled
cell division and leading to cancer. Given that Ras85D the fly
homolog of the KRAS gene, it would serve as a great model for
gene changes in Drosophila.

================================================================================
SECTION: INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

Cancer is a complex disease driven by mutations in genes
that regulate cell growth, survival, and DNA repair. One of the
most frequently mutated oncogenes in human cancers is
KRAS, which plays a critical role in controlling cell proliferation
through the RAS signaling pathway. Mutations in KRAS can
lead to tumor development and are often associated with poor
survival rates, sparking our interest in this particular oncogene.
Oncogenes are mutated genes that promote uncontrolled cell
growth. In contrast, tumor suppressor genes act to prevent
cancer by regulating cell division and repairing DNA damage.
Mismatch repair genes maintain genomic stability by
correcting replication errors and preventing the accumulation
of mutations.

Drosophila melanogaster is an ideal model system for this
purpose because it shares many conserved signaling pathways
with humans and allows for region-specific gene expression
analysis. In Drosophila, Ras85D serves as the homolog of KRAS
and is involved in similar growth and survival mechanisms. Our
research focuses on analyzing Ras85D expression across
different regions of the Drosophila midgut to identify areas of
heightened activity that could model Ras85D's role in cancer.

To better understand Ras85D's behavior, we also compared
its expression to tumor suppressors (BRCA2, APC), other
oncogenes (Pi3K92E (PIK3CA)), and mismatch repair genes
(MLH1, PMS2). This study highlights Ras85D's potential role in
tissue-specific regulation and demonstrates the usefulness of
Drosophila as a model for exploring Ras-driven cancer
pathways.

================================================================================
SECTION: METHODS
================================================================================

We used FlyBase as a primary resource to explore the GenelD, orthologs,
pathologs, pathways, and gene interactions related to the genes we
selected for our research.

The GenelD helped us examine gene expression, showing where a
specific gene is more highly expressed. We also used it to check the padj
cutoff for each gene. All of this analysis was done through SciServer
C-MOOR, with the “R" coding space given that gives us results.

For pathway analysis, we focused on KEGG pathways. We specifically
looked into cancer-related pathways, which helped us identify tumor
suppressors, oncogenes, and mismatch repair genes that are directly
involved in cancer.

The Human Protein Atlas was used to look at the protein expression in
the tissues of the body.

Our study analyzed RasB85D expression across different regions of the Drosophila midgut and found the highest transcript
levels in the Copper region, ranging from 10,000 to 12,000. This differential expression suggests that Ras85D may have a
region-specific role in regulating cell growth and survival within the Copper region. Similarly, we observed that Ras85D (KRAS)
shows high expression in the human gastrointestinal (Gl) tract, particularly in the small intestine, colon, and rectum. These
findings indicate that KRAS likely plays a critical role in Gl tissue function and may help explain its frequent mutation in
Gl-related cancers. Further research is needed to uncover the mechanisms by which KRAS contributes to tumor development
in these tissues.

In contrast, BRCA2 and APC, both tumor suppressors, were highly expressed but did not show differential expression,
indicating a more uniform role across midgut regions. PI3K92E, another oncogene involved in survival pathways, was highly
expressed but similarly lacked differential expression.

For mismatch repair genes, MLH1 and PMS2 were expressed at low levels across all regions and showed no significant
differential expression, suggesting limited activity in this tissue. Overall, Ras85D was the only gene analyzed that combined
both high expression and region-specific variation, highlighting its potential importance in localized midgut regulation and its
relevance for modeling RAS-driven pathways in Drosophila.

================================================================================
SECTION: DISCUSSION
================================================================================

KRAS is one of the most commonly mutated oncogenes in
human cancers. Normally, KRAS proteins cycle between
active and inactive states, but mutations lock KRAS in an
active form, driving uncontrolled cellular behavior that can
lead to tumor formation. Ras85D, the Drosophila homolog of
KRAS, shares similar signaling roles, making it a valuable
model for studying Ras pathways.

Our analysis revealed that Ras85D is highly expressed and
differentially regulated across midgut regions, with the
highest expression in the Copper region, suggesting that
certain midgut areas may be see more changes. Areas such
as the gastrointestinal tract are more susceptible to
mutations due to high gene expression of KRAS in humans.
In contrast, genes involved in DNA repair and tumor
suppression, such as BRCA2, APC, Pi3K92E (PIK3CA), MLH],
and PMS2, showed low or uniform expression, highlighting
Ras85D's unique regional activity.

These findings suggest that differential Ras85D expression
could model how genes expressed actively in certain regions
can contribute to cancer development in that region. Further
investigation into Ras85D mutations or overactivation in
Drosophila could provide insight into the steps of tumor
initiation and the mechanisms by which KRAS mutations
promote cancer in humans. Since Ras85D controls cell
growth, studying mutation causation to the gene, causing it
to multiply would be insightful. Ras85D serves as a powerful
tool for exploring the dynamics of Ras-signaling and its role
in driving oncogenic changes.
